# DOE Grant Writing Guide

## Agency Overview

The U.S. Department of Energy (DOE) funds research to ensure America's security and prosperity by addressing energy, environmental, and nuclear challenges. DOE is the largest federal sponsor of basic research in the physical sciences and a major funder of computational science, materials science, and clean energy technology.

### Key Offices and Their Missions

**Office of Science (SC)**
- Largest funder of basic research in the physical sciences in the U.S.
- Six program offices: Basic Energy Sciences (BES), Advanced Scientific Computing Research (ASCR), Biological and Environmental Research (BER), Fusion Energy Sciences (FES), High Energy Physics (HEP), Nuclear Physics (NP)
- Supports fundamental research, national user facilities, and large-scale scientific instruments
- Typical awards: $150K--$500K/year for 3 years (single PI); up to $2M+/year for Energy Frontier Research Centers (EFRCs)

**ARPA-E (Advanced Research Projects Agency--Energy)**
- High-risk, high-reward energy technology development
- Focus on technologies that can be transformative and commercially viable
- Requires clear commercialization pathway and techno-economic analysis
- Typical awards: $500K--$10M over 2--3 years
- Strong cost-sharing requirements (typically 20% or more for large organizations)

**EERE (Energy Efficiency and Renewable Energy)**
- Applied research and development in renewable energy, energy efficiency, and sustainable transportation
- Close ties to industry; technology readiness level (TRL) advancement is central
- Programs include Solar Energy Technologies, Wind Energy Technologies, Vehicle Technologies, Building Technologies, Hydrogen and Fuel Cell Technologies
- Typical awards: $500K--$5M; often requires industry partnership and cost sharing

## Proposal Structure (Typical DOE FOA Response)

Most DOE Funding Opportunity Announcements (FOAs) require the following components:

### 1. Project Narrative (10--25 pages, varies by FOA)
- **Background and Significance**: Problem statement, current state of the art, gaps addressed
- **Technical Approach**: Detailed description of methods, models, experiments
- **Innovation and Impact**: What is new, why it matters for DOE's mission
- **Preliminary Results**: Data demonstrating feasibility
- **Project Management and Timeline**: Milestones, decision points, Gantt chart
- **Roles and Responsibilities**: Team expertise and task assignments
- **Facilities and Resources**: Equipment, computing, lab capabilities

### 2. Budget and Budget Justification
- Detailed budget by year and by task/phase
- Budget justification for every line item
- Cost-sharing documentation (if required)
- Subcontract budgets with separate justifications

### 3. Supporting Documents
- Biographical sketches (DOE format, typically 2 pages each)
- Current and pending support
- Letters of commitment from collaborators and national labs
- Data management plan
- Institutional commitment letters (for cost sharing)

## Review Criteria

DOE proposals are generally evaluated on five criteria, though exact weighting varies by FOA:

1. **Scientific and/or Technical Merit** (typically 30--40%)
   - Quality and innovation of the proposed research
   - Soundness of the technical approach
   - Adequacy of preliminary data

2. **Appropriateness of the Proposed Method or Approach** (typically 20--30%)
   - Feasibility of the research plan
   - Logical progression of tasks
   - Adequacy of contingency plans

3. **Competency of Personnel and Adequacy of Facilities** (typically 15--20%)
   - PI and team qualifications
   - Access to necessary equipment and infrastructure
   - Track record of productivity

4. **Reasonableness and Appropriateness of Budget** (typically 10--15%)
   - Alignment of budget with proposed work
   - Justification of major expenses
   - Cost efficiency

5. **Relevance to DOE Mission and Program Goals** (typically 10--20%)
   - Alignment with the specific FOA objectives
   - Contribution to DOE strategic priorities
   - Potential for broader energy impact

## Budget Rules and Requirements

### Cost Sharing
- **ARPA-E**: Typically requires 20%+ cost sharing from non-federal sources for large entities; reduced for small businesses and universities
- **EERE**: Cost sharing requirements vary by FOA (often 20--50% for later-stage technology)
- **Office of Science**: Generally does not require cost sharing for basic research
- Cost sharing must be documented with commitment letters and verifiable

### Budget Structure
- **Quarterly budgets**: Many DOE programs require quarterly spending projections
- **Phase-based budgets**: ARPA-E and EERE often require budgets broken out by project phase
- **Task-based budgets**: Budget must map to specific tasks in the project management plan
- **National lab collaboration**: Costs for national lab partners are typically handled through a separate Work Authorization System (WAS) or Field Work Proposal (FWP); these appear as a distinct budget category

### Key Budget Items
- **Indirect costs**: Use your institution's federally negotiated rate
- **Equipment**: Items over $5,000; must be justified as essential
- **Travel**: Include DOE program review meetings (typically 1--2 per year in Washington, D.C.)
- **Subcontracts**: First $25K of each subcontract subject to full indirect; remainder at reduced rate (check your institution's policy)

## Common Mistakes

1. **Ignoring DOE mission alignment**: Proposals that read like pure academic curiosity without connecting to energy, environment, or national security goals
2. **Weak project management plan**: DOE values clear milestones, go/no-go decision points, and quarterly deliverables more than most agencies
3. **Underestimating cost-sharing requirements**: Failing to secure and document matching funds before submission (especially ARPA-E and EERE)
4. **No commercialization pathway (ARPA-E/EERE)**: Applied programs require techno-economic analysis and a credible path to market
5. **Ignoring national lab opportunities**: DOE reviewers look favorably on leveraging national lab facilities and expertise; not considering this is a missed opportunity
6. **Vague preliminary data**: DOE reviewers want clear evidence of feasibility, not just published papers in the general area
7. **Budget-narrative misalignment**: Budget items that do not map to specific tasks in the narrative
8. **Missing required elements**: Each FOA has specific requirements; check the checklist carefully (page limits, font sizes, required sections)
9. **Generic broader impacts**: DOE cares about workforce development and technology transfer, not generic outreach statements
10. **Late engagement with program managers**: Unlike NSF, DOE program managers often welcome pre-submission conversations; not reaching out is a strategic error

## Tips for Computational-Experimental Integration Proposals

DOE strongly values proposals that combine computational and experimental approaches. This is particularly true for Basic Energy Sciences, ASCR partnerships, and many ARPA-E programs.

### Framing the Integration
- Clearly articulate why both computational and experimental components are necessary -- not just convenient
- Show a feedback loop: computation guides experiments, experimental results validate and refine models
- Demonstrate that neither approach alone would achieve the objectives

### Structuring the Proposal
- Define clear handoff points between computational and experimental tasks
- Include milestones that require integration (e.g., "Model predictions validated by experimental measurement of X")
- Show the team has genuine expertise in both areas, not just a token collaborator

### Computational Components
- Specify the codes, algorithms, and computing resources (DOE leadership computing facilities like NERSC, OLCF, or ALCF are strong assets)
- Include benchmarking and validation plans
- Address scalability and computational cost
- If using machine learning or AI, explain the training data source and validation strategy

### Experimental Components
- Describe how experimental data will be structured for computational use
- Plan for data formats, metadata standards, and sharing
- If using DOE user facilities (synchrotrons, neutron sources, nanoscience centers), mention approved or pending proposals for beam time

### Budget Considerations
- Justify computational resources (INCITE or ALCC allocations are free but should be mentioned)
- Include funding for data management and sharing infrastructure
- Budget for integration activities: joint team meetings, cross-training, shared postdocs

### What Reviewers Look For
- Genuine integration, not two separate projects stapled together
- Clear scientific questions that require both approaches
- A management plan that ensures computational and experimental teams communicate regularly
- Prior evidence of successful computational-experimental collaboration by the team

## Additional Strategic Tips

- **Contact the program manager**: DOE program managers are often willing to discuss whether your idea fits a specific FOA. A 15-minute conversation can save months of wasted effort.
- **Leverage DOE user facilities**: Mention access to national labs, light sources, computing centers, and other DOE-funded infrastructure.
- **Attend PI meetings**: If you have existing DOE funding, attend annual PI meetings to understand program direction and network.
- **Watch for targeted FOAs**: DOE frequently releases narrowly scoped FOAs; the best fit wins. Monitor the DOE Office of Science and ARPA-E websites regularly.
- **Early Career Program**: Junior faculty within 10 years of PhD should strongly consider the DOE Early Career Research Program -- it is prestigious and provides 5 years of stable funding.
- **EFRCs and Hubs**: For large collaborative efforts, watch for Energy Frontier Research Center and Energy Innovation Hub solicitations.
- **Resubmission**: DOE does not have a formal resubmission process like NIH, but you can submit a revised proposal to a new FOA. Incorporate feedback from prior reviews.

- [ ] Confirm eligibility (institution type, cost sharing capacity)

- [ ] Assemble team with complementary expertise
- [ ] Secure national lab collaborator letters (if applicable)
- [ ] Prepare cost-sharing documentation (if required)
- [ ] Write narrative with clear DOE mission alignment
- [ ] Develop detailed project management plan with quarterly milestones
- [ ] Create task-based budget matching the narrative
- [ ] Include data management plan
- [ ] Review page limits, font requirements, and required sections
- [ ] Submit through your institution's sponsored research office
- [ ] Submit at least 48 hours before the deadline (DOE portals can be slow)
