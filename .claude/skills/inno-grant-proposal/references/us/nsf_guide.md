# NSF (National Science Foundation) Proposal Guide

## Agency Overview

**Mission**: Promote the progress of science; advance national health, prosperity, and welfare; secure the national defense.

NSF funds approximately 25% of all federally supported basic research at U.S. colleges and universities. With an annual budget of approximately $8.7-9.0 billion (subject to annual Congressional appropriations), NSF supports research across all fields of science and engineering (except medical sciences, which fall under NIH). NSF does not operate its own laboratories -- every dollar goes to external researchers and institutions.

**Current Strategic Priorities**:
- Artificial intelligence and quantum information science
- Climate and clean energy research
- Biotechnology and biomanufacturing
- Advanced manufacturing and semiconductors
- Broadening participation in STEM
- Research security and international collaboration

**What Sets NSF Apart**:
- Equal weighting of Intellectual Merit and Broader Impacts (unique among federal agencies)
- Strong emphasis on education, training, and diversity
- Support for fundamental, curiosity-driven research
- Merit review by panels of peer scientists
- Program officers who actively shape research directions

## Program Types and Funding Ranges

### Standard Research Grants
- **Budget**: Varies by directorate; typically $100K-$500K total
- **Duration**: 1-5 years (3 years is most common)
- **Best for**: Established research questions with clear methodology
- **Note**: Budget norms vary widely by field -- check your program's typical award size

### CAREER Awards (Faculty Early Career Development)
- **Budget**: Minimum $400K over 5 years ($500K in some directorates like BIO and ENG)
- **Duration**: Exactly 5 years
- **Eligibility**: Untenured tenure-track faculty; see detailed section below
- **Best for**: Building a research program with integrated education

### Collaborative Research
- **Budget**: Split across institutions; each submits separately
- **Duration**: Typically 3-5 years
- **Structure**: One lead PI coordinates; each institution submits its own proposal with shared project description
- **Key requirement**: Clearly justify why collaboration is necessary, not just convenient

### RAPID (Rapid Response Research)
- **Budget**: Up to $200K
- **Duration**: Up to 1 year (non-renewable)
- **Best for**: Urgent research on natural disasters, pandemics, or fleeting phenomena
- **No external review**: Program officer makes the decision
- **No preliminary data required**: Speed is the priority

### EAGER (Early-concept Grants for Exploratory Research)
- **Budget**: Up to $300K
- **Duration**: Up to 2 years
- **Best for**: Untested, high-risk ideas that lack preliminary data
- **By invitation or pre-approval**: Contact your program officer first
- **No external review**: Program officer decides based on transformative potential

## Proposal Structure and Requirements

### 1. Project Summary (1 page, strict limit)

Three clearly labeled sections, each as a separate paragraph:

**Overview**: What you propose to do and why it matters. Write this for an educated non-specialist. State the problem, your approach, and expected outcomes in accessible language.

**Intellectual Merit**: What new knowledge or understanding will this work generate? How does it advance the field? Address the quality of the research plan and team qualifications.

**Broader Impacts**: How will this work benefit society beyond the research itself? Be specific about activities, not vague about aspirations.

**Practical tips**:
- This is the first thing reviewers read -- make it compelling
- Many reviewers form initial impressions here that color their reading of everything else
- Write it last, after the project description is complete
- Avoid jargon; a reviewer outside your subfield will read this
- Each section should stand alone as a coherent paragraph

### 2. Project Description (15 pages maximum)

This is the core of your proposal. Typical structure:

**Introduction and Motivation** (1-2 pages)
- Open with a clear statement of the problem and why it matters now
- Establish the gap in knowledge your work will fill
- State your central hypothesis or research questions
- Provide a roadmap of what follows

**Background and Literature Review** (2-3 pages)
- Demonstrate command of the field without being exhaustive
- Focus on work directly relevant to your proposed research
- Identify the specific gap your work addresses
- Show how your approach differs from and improves upon prior work

**Preliminary Results** (1-2 pages, if applicable)
- Present data that demonstrates feasibility
- Not strictly required by NSF, but strengthens competitive proposals
- Especially important for novel or high-risk approaches
- Use figures effectively -- a strong figure saves words and builds confidence

**Research Plan** (5-7 pages)
- Organize by aims, objectives, or research thrusts
- For each aim: rationale, methods, expected outcomes, potential pitfalls, alternative approaches
- Be specific enough to judge feasibility but not so detailed that you lose the forest
- Include a timeline (Gantt chart recommended)
- Address how you will handle negative or unexpected results

**Broader Impacts** (1-2 pages)
- Integrate throughout AND include a dedicated section
- Describe specific, concrete activities with measurable outcomes
- Connect to your research (not tacked-on outreach)
- Show institutional support and existing partnerships

**Results from Prior NSF Support** (up to 1 page per prior award)
- Required if PI received NSF funding in the past 5 years
- Include: award number, amount, period, title, summary of results, publications, students trained
- This is your track record -- make it count

### 3. References Cited
- No page limit, but does not count toward the 15-page project description
- Use consistent formatting
- Cite comprehensively but strategically -- show breadth of knowledge
- Include your own relevant publications

### 4. Budget and Budget Justification
- Detailed budget by year using NSF's budget template
- Separate justification document explaining each line item
- See Budget Rules section below for specific constraints

### 5. Biographical Sketch (no page limit; must use SciENcv common form)
- Must be prepared using SciENcv (the NSF fillable PDF is no longer accepted)
- Professional preparation, appointments, products (up to 10)
- "Products" replaces "publications" -- you can list software, datasets, patents
- **Synergistic Activities** is now a SEPARATE 1-page document (not part of the biosketch)

### 6. Current and Pending Support
- All current and pending funding for all senior personnel
- Include in-kind support and foreign funding
- Use the NSF-approved format
- Failure to disclose is a compliance violation with serious consequences

### 7. Data Management and Sharing Plan (DMSP) (2 pages maximum)
- How data will be collected, stored, and shared
- What standards and formats you will use
- Plans for long-term preservation and access
- Increasingly important in review -- do not treat as an afterthought

### 8. Postdoctoral Mentoring Plan (1 page, if applicable)
- **Required** if the proposal requests funding for postdoctoral researchers
- Describe mentoring activities: career counseling, training in research ethics, professional development, publication/presentation opportunities
- Must be a separate document (not embedded in the project description)

### 9. Facilities, Equipment, and Other Resources
- No page limit
- Describe institutional resources available
- Do not include financial commitments here (those go in budget)
- Demonstrate you have what you need to succeed

## Review Criteria (Detailed)

NSF uses two equally weighted review criteria. Reviewers assign ratings of Excellent, Very Good, Good, Fair, or Poor for each.

### Intellectual Merit

Reviewers consider:
- **Potential to advance knowledge**: Does this push the field forward in meaningful ways?
- **Quality of research plan**: Is the approach well-conceived, organized, and feasible?
- **Creativity and originality**: Does this challenge existing paradigms or offer new approaches?
- **Qualifications of the team**: Does the PI/team have the expertise and track record?
- **Adequacy of resources**: Are the facilities, equipment, and institutional support sufficient?

**How to strengthen Intellectual Merit**:
- State explicitly what is novel about your approach
- Provide a logical chain from problem to approach to expected outcomes
- Include preliminary data when possible
- Demonstrate deep knowledge of the field through targeted citations
- Address feasibility and risk head-on

### Broader Impacts

NSF defines five categories. Address at least one substantively (two or three is better):

**1. Advancing discovery while promoting teaching, training, and learning**
- Integrate research into courses or curriculum
- Train graduate and undergraduate students through the research
- Develop educational materials or workshops
- Examples: new lab modules, research experiences for undergraduates (REU), graduate student mentoring plans

**2. Broadening participation of underrepresented groups**
- Recruit students from underrepresented backgrounds
- Partner with minority-serving institutions (MSIs, HBCUs, HSIs)
- Create mentoring or bridge programs
- Examples: summer research programs for community college students, partnerships with tribal colleges

**3. Enhancing infrastructure for research and education**
- Develop shared instruments, databases, or software
- Create cyberinfrastructure or community resources
- Build multi-institutional networks
- Examples: open-source tools, shared datasets, community standards

**4. Broad dissemination to enhance scientific understanding**
- Public lectures, museum exhibits, media engagement
- K-12 outreach programs
- Science communication training
- Examples: citizen science projects, YouTube explainers, school visit programs

**5. Benefits to society**
- Environmental, health, economic, or security applications
- Policy-relevant findings
- Workforce development
- Examples: clean water technology, disaster resilience tools, industry partnerships

**Critical advice on Broader Impacts**:
- Generic statements ("we will mentor students") score poorly
- Include a timeline, specific activities, and how you will measure success
- Leverage existing institutional programs rather than inventing from scratch
- Show that you have already started (e.g., current outreach partnerships)
- Budget for your broader impacts activities

## Budget Rules and Guidelines

### Faculty Salary
- **Maximum 2 months of summer salary** per year from a single NSF award
- Academic-year salary support is allowed but uncommon (requires course buyout justification)
- If you have multiple NSF awards, the 2-month cap applies across all of them combined
- Include fringe benefits at your institution's negotiated rate

### Graduate Student Support
- NSF strongly encourages supporting graduate students
- Include tuition, stipend, and health insurance
- Check your institution's current rates -- these change annually
- Graduate students are often the strongest budget justification

### Equipment
- Items costing **$5,000 or more per unit** are classified as equipment
- Equipment is excluded from indirect cost calculations (saving money)
- Must be justified as essential to the research
- Items under $5,000 go under Materials and Supplies

### Travel
- Domestic and international travel for conferences, collaboration, fieldwork
- Justify each trip's purpose and relevance to the project
- One domestic conference per year per person is standard
- Include student travel to conferences (strengthens training narrative)

### Indirect Costs (F&A)
- Use your institution's federally negotiated rate
- Applied to modified total direct costs (MTDC)
- Equipment, tuition, and subaward amounts over $25K are typically excluded from MTDC
- You cannot reduce your indirect rate to fit a budget cap

### Subawards
- First $25K of each subaward is subject to your institution's indirect costs
- Amounts above $25K are excluded from your indirect cost base
- Each subaward institution needs its own budget and justification
- Include a letter of collaboration from each subaward PI

### Cost Sharing
- NSF **generally prohibits** voluntary committed cost sharing
- Do not offer cost sharing unless specifically required by the solicitation
- Mandatory cost sharing is rare and program-specific

### Letters of Collaboration vs. Letters of Support
- **Letters of collaboration**: Permitted; must use a formulaic format (e.g., "If the proposal is funded, I will collaborate with [PI] on [specific activity]"). Do not include endorsements or evaluative language.
- **Letters of support**: Prohibited by NSF. Proposals including letters that go beyond the standard collaboration language may be returned without review.

## CAREER Award Specifics

The CAREER award is NSF's most prestigious award for junior faculty, supporting those who integrate research and education.

### Eligibility Requirements
- Hold a tenure-track (or equivalent) position at a U.S. institution
- Have not previously received a CAREER award
- Untenured at time of submission (tenured faculty are ineligible)
- Hold a doctoral degree
- Must be in a position that includes both research and education responsibilities
- Typically within the first few years of appointment (check directorate-specific guidance)

### What Makes CAREER Different
- Research and education must be **genuinely integrated**, not parallel tracks
- Five-year duration allows for ambitious, developmental research programs
- Minimum $400K ($500K in BIO and ENG directorates)
- Reviewed by disciplinary panels, same as regular proposals
- Submission deadline is the fourth Wednesday in July annually (one shot per year)

### Proposal Requirements
- Standard NSF proposal components plus:
- **Department letter**: Chair must confirm tenure-track status and support
- **Education plan**: Must be woven throughout the project description, not a separate appendix
- **Integration**: The education activities should flow from and reinforce the research

### Keys to a Competitive CAREER Proposal
- Start with a bold research vision that will define your career trajectory
- Design education activities that are natural extensions of the research
- Show how the research enables unique educational experiences
- Demonstrate institutional support for both research and education components
- Include assessment plan for educational activities
- Build on existing departmental or institutional education infrastructure

### Common CAREER Pitfalls
- Treating education as an add-on rather than an integral component
- Proposing a research plan identical to a standard grant (no career vision)
- Being too conservative -- this is meant to launch an ambitious program
- Neglecting to explain how the research and education reinforce each other
- Submitting to the wrong program (confirm with your program officer)

## Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

### 1. Weak Broader Impacts
**Mistake**: Vague, last-minute statements like "results will be disseminated through publications."
**Fix**: Plan specific, measurable activities with timelines. Budget for them. Start partnerships before submitting.

### 2. Exceeding Page Limits
**Mistake**: Squeezing text with small margins or fonts to fit.
**Fix**: NSF uses automated compliance checks. Violations cause desk rejection. Write concisely instead.

### 3. Misalignment Between Budget and Narrative
**Mistake**: Proposing activities in the project description that are not supported in the budget (or vice versa).
**Fix**: Cross-check every activity against the budget. If you mention travel, equipment, or personnel, it must appear in both documents.

### 4. Ignoring the "Prior Support" Section
**Mistake**: Treating it as a formality rather than an opportunity to demonstrate track record.
**Fix**: Highlight productivity, student training, publications, and how prior work leads to the current proposal.

### 5. Overly Ambitious Scope
**Mistake**: Proposing three projects worth of work in one proposal.
**Fix**: One coherent research program with 2-4 well-defined aims. Reviewers prefer depth over breadth.

### 6. Missing the Program Fit
**Mistake**: Submitting to a program whose priorities do not match your research.
**Fix**: Read the program description carefully. Email the program officer to confirm fit before writing.

### 7. Neglecting Feasibility
**Mistake**: Proposing novel methods without evidence they will work.
**Fix**: Include preliminary data, cite published validation, or propose a phased approach with early feasibility checks.

### 8. Poor Figure Quality
**Mistake**: Low-resolution figures, tiny labels, or figures that do not stand alone.
**Fix**: Every figure should be readable in black and white, have complete captions, and convey information that text alone cannot.

### 9. Not Contacting the Program Officer
**Mistake**: Guessing about program fit, budget norms, or submission timing.
**Fix**: A 15-minute call with the PO can save months of wasted effort. They want to fund good science -- help them help you.

### 10. Rushing the Data Management and Sharing Plan (DMSP)
**Mistake**: Copy-pasting a generic DMP that does not address your specific data.
**Fix**: Tailor it to your project. Describe your actual data types, volumes, formats, and sharing timeline.

## Resubmission Strategy

NSF does not have a formal resubmission mechanism like NIH's A1. A declined proposal can be revised and resubmitted as a new proposal, reviewed by the same or a different panel.

### After a Decline

**Step 1: Read reviews carefully and without defensiveness.** Wait 48 hours before responding emotionally. Look for consensus criticisms versus outlier opinions.

**Step 2: Request a debrief from the program officer.** They can share panel discussion context that goes beyond written reviews. Ask:
- What were the main concerns in panel discussion?
- Is this worth resubmitting to the same program?
- Would a different program be a better fit?

**Step 3: Categorize the criticisms.**
- **Fatal flaws** (conceptual problems, wrong program fit): Require fundamental rethinking
- **Fixable weaknesses** (unclear methods, missing preliminary data): Address directly
- **Misunderstandings** (reviewer missed something): Improve clarity so the next panel cannot miss it

**Step 4: Revise strategically.**
- Address every substantive concern, even if you disagree
- If a reviewer misunderstood, the writing was unclear -- fix the writing
- Add new preliminary data if feasibility was questioned
- Strengthen the weakest section rather than polishing the strongest
- Have someone outside your field read the revision

**Step 5: Consider timing.**
- Some panels meet once per year; missing the cycle means a 12-month delay
- A stronger proposal submitted 6 months later beats a rushed revision

### Success Rates
- First submissions have lower success rates (~20-25% across NSF)
- Revised resubmissions fare significantly better when criticisms are substantively addressed
- Many funded proposals were declined at least once before funding

## Writing Tips for Competitive Proposals

### Write for Tired Reviewers
Panel members read 15-25 proposals in a few days. Your proposal must be scannable, clear, and compelling even at 10 PM after a long day of reviews.

- **Use descriptive headings** that convey content, not just structure. "Aim 2: Machine Learning for Protein Folding Prediction" beats "Aim 2."
- **Lead every paragraph with a topic sentence** that states the point. Reviewers who skim should still grasp your argument.
- **Use bold text strategically** to highlight key claims, but do not overdo it.
- **White space matters.** Dense blocks of text are hard to read. Use paragraph breaks, bullet lists, and figures.

### Choose Strong, Active Language
- **Active voice**: "We will develop a new algorithm" not "A new algorithm will be developed"
- **Strong verbs**: "investigate," "elucidate," "quantify," "demonstrate" instead of "look at," "study," "try to understand"
- **Confident but not arrogant**: "Our preliminary data demonstrate..." not "Obviously, our results prove..."
- **Precise language**: "Three samples per condition" not "Several samples." Specific numbers build credibility.

### Build a Persuasive Narrative Arc
1. **Hook**: Open with a compelling problem statement that establishes urgency
2. **Gap**: Show what is not known and why current approaches fall short
3. **Bridge**: Introduce your approach as the logical solution
4. **Evidence**: Support with preliminary data and literature
5. **Vision**: Close sections with the transformative potential of success

### Make Figures Work Hard
- A well-designed conceptual figure on page 1 can frame the entire proposal
- Gantt charts demonstrate planning and feasibility at a glance
- Preliminary data figures build confidence more effectively than text descriptions
- Every figure should be referenced in the text and have a self-contained caption

### Avoid These Language Traps
- **"Clearly" and "obviously"**: If it were clear, you would not need to say so. These words irritate reviewers.
- **"Novel" without evidence**: Claiming novelty requires demonstrating knowledge of prior work.
- **"We hope to" or "We will try to"**: These signal uncertainty. State what you will do, then acknowledge risks separately.
- **Acronym overload**: Define each acronym once; avoid using more than 8-10 in a proposal.
- **Passive constructions**: They obscure responsibility and weaken your narrative.

### The One-Page Test
After drafting, ask a colleague outside your field to read only the Project Summary and the first page of the Project Description. If they cannot tell you (1) what problem you are solving, (2) why it matters, and (3) what you plan to do about it, revise until they can.

## Quick Reference Checklist

Before submission, verify:

- [ ] Project Summary has three labeled sections (Overview, Intellectual Merit, Broader Impacts)
- [ ] Project Description is within 15 pages
- [ ] All figures are legible and referenced in text
- [ ] Budget aligns with all activities described in the narrative
- [ ] Faculty salary does not exceed 2 months summer support
- [ ] Equipment items over $5K are listed as equipment (not supplies)
- [ ] Data Management and Sharing Plan (DMSP) is tailored to your project (not generic)
- [ ] Biographical Sketches use NSF-approved format
- [ ] Current and Pending Support is complete and accurate for all senior personnel
- [ ] Prior NSF Support section is included (if applicable)
- [ ] References are complete and consistently formatted
- [ ] Broader Impacts are specific, measurable, and budgeted
- [ ] Program officer has been contacted to confirm fit
- [ ] A colleague outside your field has read the summary and first pages
- [ ] Submission is planned for 48+ hours before the deadline
