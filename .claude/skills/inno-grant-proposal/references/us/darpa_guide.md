# DARPA Grant Writing Guide

## Agency Overview

The Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency (DARPA) creates breakthrough technologies and capabilities for national security. Founded in 1958 in response to Sputnik, DARPA's mission is to "make pivotal investments in breakthrough technologies for national security."

DARPA operates fundamentally differently from other federal agencies. It is not a basic research funder -- it invests in high-risk, high-reward technology development with clear transition paths to military or intelligence applications. The guiding question for every DARPA program is: **"What if true, who cares?"** -- meaning, if this technology works, does it change the game for national security?

### Key Characteristics
- Program-manager driven: Individual PMs define programs, set goals, and select performers
- Short program lifecycles: Most programs run 3--5 years
- Milestone-driven: Quantitative metrics and go/no-go decision points
- Prototypes over papers: Demonstrations and working systems matter more than publications
- High risk tolerance: DARPA expects some projects to fail; that is by design
- Rapid contracting: Moves faster than most government agencies

## Office Structure

DARPA is organized into six technical offices, each with distinct focus areas:

**BTO -- Biological Technologies Office**
- Bio-inspired and bio-derived technologies
- Human performance, disease prevention, biosecurity
- Neurotechnology and brain-computer interfaces
- Synthetic biology and engineered organisms

**DSO -- Defense Sciences Office**
- Fundamental science that could lead to breakthrough capabilities
- Mathematics, physics, materials science, social science
- Most "basic research"-oriented office
- Novel materials, quantum science, complex social systems

**I2O -- Information Innovation Office**
- Artificial intelligence and machine learning
- Cyber security and resilience
- Large-scale data analytics
- Human-machine teaming and decision support

**MTO -- Microsystems Technology Office**
- Advanced electronics and photonics
- Microelectromechanical systems (MEMS)
- Novel computing architectures
- Sensing and communication systems

**STO -- Strategic Technology Office**
- Battle management and command-and-control
- Communications and networking
- Electronic warfare and sensing
- Space systems and contested environments

**TTO -- Tactical Technology Office**
- Autonomous systems and robotics
- Advanced weapons systems
- Ground, maritime, and air platforms
- Rapid prototyping and manufacturing

## Program Types

### Broad Agency Announcements (BAAs)
The primary solicitation mechanism for DARPA. Each BAA describes a specific technical challenge and invites proposals.

- **Program-specific BAAs**: Detailed solicitations for defined programs with specific technical areas (TAs)
- **Office-wide BAAs**: Open solicitations accepting innovative ideas within an office's scope
- **HR001120S0019 (perpetual)**: DARPA's "open door" for revolutionary ideas outside current programs

### Award Types
- **Contracts**: Most common for large programs; can be cost-plus-fixed-fee, cost-plus-incentive-fee, or firm-fixed-price
- **Cooperative Agreements**: When DARPA wants substantial involvement in the research
- **Grants**: Less common; typically for basic research at universities
- **Other Transactions (OTs)**: Flexible agreements that bypass some federal acquisition regulations; increasingly popular

### Special Programs
- **Young Faculty Award (YFA)**: For tenure-track faculty within first 5 years; up to $500K over 2 years with potential $500K Director's Fellowship extension
- **Director's Fellowship**: Extension of YFA for exceptional performers
- **DARPA Forward**: Engagement events at universities to introduce researchers to DARPA
- **Embedded Entrepreneurship Initiative (EEI)**: Supports transition of DARPA technologies to commercial markets

## Proposal Structure

DARPA proposals typically follow a multi-phase structure. Most BAAs require an initial abstract or executive summary before a full proposal.

### Phase 1: Abstract / Executive Summary (2--5 pages)
- Technical challenge you are addressing
- Proposed innovation and approach
- Key team members and qualifications
- Rough cost and schedule estimate
- Why this matters for national security

### Phase 2: Full Proposal (if invited)
A full DARPA proposal generally includes:

**Volume I -- Technical and Management (25--50 pages typical)**
1. **Executive Summary** (1--2 pages)
2. **Technical Approach** (core of the proposal)
   - Problem statement and current limitations
   - Proposed innovation and technical approach
   - Comparison with state of the art
   - Preliminary results or proof of concept
3. **Schedule and Milestones**
   - Multi-phase structure (Phase 1: feasibility, Phase 2: development, Phase 3: demonstration)
   - Quarterly milestones with quantitative metrics
   - Go/no-go decision criteria between phases
   - Deliverables for each phase
4. **Risk Assessment and Mitigation**
   - Technical risks ranked by likelihood and impact
   - Mitigation strategies for each risk
   - Alternative approaches if primary approach fails
5. **Team and Management**
   - Organizational chart and roles
   - Key personnel qualifications
   - Teaming arrangements and subcontractors
   - Management approach and communication plan

**Volume II -- Cost Proposal**
- Detailed cost breakdown by phase and task
- Labor categories, rates, and hours
- Materials, equipment, travel, subcontracts
- Supporting cost documentation
- Basis of estimate for all major items

**Volume III -- Administrative**
- Certifications and representations
- Organizational conflict of interest statements
- Security considerations (if classified)
- Intellectual property assertions

## The "What If True, Who Cares" Principle

This is the single most important framing concept for DARPA proposals. Your proposal must answer three questions convincingly:

1. **What if it's true?** -- If your technology works as proposed, what capability does it create? Be specific and quantitative.

2. **Who cares?** -- Which military service, intelligence agency, or national security mission would benefit? Name the customer. Vague "could be useful for defense" statements are insufficient.

3. **Why now?** -- What has changed (new science, new data, new threat) that makes this feasible and urgent today when it was not possible before?

If you cannot answer all three compellingly, the proposal is not ready for DARPA.

## Review Criteria

DARPA evaluates proposals on three primary criteria:

### 1. Overall Scientific and Technical Merit
- Innovation and novelty of the approach
- Technical feasibility and soundness
- Quality and relevance of preliminary data
- Potential for transformative impact (not incremental improvement)
- Clear quantitative metrics for success

### 2. Potential Contribution to the DARPA Mission
- Relevance to national security needs
- Magnitude of potential capability improvement
- Transition pathway to operational use
- Differentiation from existing or planned programs
- Alignment with the specific BAA objectives

### 3. Realism of Proposed Costs and Schedule
- Cost realism (not lowest cost -- realistic cost)
- Schedule feasibility with appropriate margins
- Appropriateness of personnel and resource allocation
- Reasonableness of subcontractor costs
- Cost efficiency relative to proposed deliverables

**Note**: Unlike NSF or NIH, DARPA does not use numeric scores. Evaluation is holistic, and the program manager has significant discretion.

## Budget Rules

### Structure
- **Phase-based**: Budget must be broken out by program phase (typically 2--3 phases)
- **Task-based**: Within each phase, costs are organized by technical task
- **Contract types**: Cost-plus-fixed-fee (most common for R&D), firm-fixed-price (for well-defined deliverables), or other transactions

### Key Requirements
- **Travel**: Budget for quarterly program reviews (typically in Arlington, VA) and annual demonstrations
- **Government-furnished equipment/information**: Identify what DARPA or DoD will provide vs. what you must procure
- **Labor rates**: Must be consistent with your institution's established rates; DARPA will verify
- **Subcontractors**: Need detailed cost breakdowns; prime is responsible for sub performance
- **Intellectual property**: Assertions about background IP and planned IP must be explicit

### Cost Realism vs. Cost Minimization
DARPA evaluates cost realism, not lowest cost. Underbidding is a red flag -- it signals that you do not understand the scope of work. Budget what you actually need, and justify it.

## DARPA-Specific Tips

### 1. Prototypes Beat Papers
DARPA funds technology development, not academic inquiry. Frame your proposal around what you will build, demonstrate, and deliver -- not what you will study or publish.

### 2. High-Risk, High-Reward Framing
Do not shy away from ambitious claims, but back them with a credible technical approach. DARPA wants to hear "this could change everything" -- as long as you can explain how.

### 3. Milestone-Driven Planning
Every phase should have quantitative go/no-go milestones. Example: "Phase 1 success = demonstration of X at Y performance level in Z conditions." Vague milestones like "complete analysis" are weak.

### 4. Know the Program Manager
DARPA is PM-driven. Read the BAA carefully to understand the PM's vision. Attend Proposers' Days. If possible, have a brief conversation with the PM before submitting.

### 5. Team Composition
DARPA values teams that combine academic innovation with industry execution capability. A university-only team may lack credibility for system-level demonstrations. Consider partnering with defense contractors, national labs, or startups.

### 6. Transition Planning
Address how the technology will move from your lab to operational use. Who is the transition partner? What is the path from prototype to fielded system? Proposals that end at "proof of concept" are less competitive.

### 7. Security Awareness
Some DARPA programs involve classified information. Ensure your facility has appropriate clearances or plan for how to handle classified components. Even unclassified programs may become classified as they mature.

### 8. Respond to Exactly What Is Asked
DARPA BAAs are precise. If the BAA asks for a specific capability at a specific performance level, address that directly. Do not substitute your own problem statement for the one DARPA defined.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Academic framing**: Writing a proposal that reads like an NSF submission -- focused on advancing knowledge rather than creating capability
2. **Incremental innovation**: Proposing a 10% improvement when DARPA wants 10x. If it is not a game-changer, it is not DARPA-appropriate
3. **No transition path**: Failing to identify who will use the technology and how it gets from lab to field
4. **Ignoring the BAA structure**: Not organizing the proposal around the Technical Areas and metrics defined in the BAA
5. **Weak milestones**: Vague or non-quantitative success criteria that do not allow objective go/no-go decisions
6. **Underbidding**: Proposing an unrealistically low budget to appear competitive; DARPA evaluates realism, not lowest cost
7. **Solo PI proposals**: DARPA problems typically require multi-disciplinary teams; a single-investigator proposal rarely has sufficient breadth
8. **Missing Proposers' Day**: Not attending the Proposers' Day event, which provides critical context and teaming opportunities
9. **Ignoring the "who cares" question**: Brilliant technology with no clear national security application
10. **Poor risk management**: Either ignoring risks entirely or being so cautious that the proposal lacks ambition

## Proposal Development Timeline

### 6--8 Weeks Before Deadline
- Read BAA thoroughly (multiple times)
- Attend Proposers' Day
- Identify teaming partners
- Develop initial concept and approach
- Contact PM if questions arise

### 4--6 Weeks Before Deadline
- Submit abstract (if required)
- Receive feedback or invitation to submit full proposal
- Draft technical approach and schedule
- Begin cost estimation with team

### 2--4 Weeks Before Deadline
- Complete technical volume draft
- Develop detailed cost proposal
- Internal review by colleagues with DARPA experience
- Refine milestones and metrics

### Final 2 Weeks
- Final revisions and compliance check
- Institutional review and approval
- Format verification (page limits, fonts, required sections)
- Submit at least 48 hours early

## Young Faculty Award (YFA) Specific Guidance

The YFA is DARPA's investment in the next generation of researchers. Key points:

- **Eligibility**: Tenure-track faculty within first 5 years of appointment; U.S. citizens or permanent residents at U.S. institutions
- **Funding**: Up to $500K over 2 years; potential $500K Director's Fellowship extension for top performers
- **Scope**: Propose a focused research effort aligned with DARPA office interests
- **Mentorship**: DARPA assigns a PM mentor; regular interaction expected
- **Tips**: Be bold but focused; demonstrate understanding of defense applications; show a clear 2-year plan with achievable milestones
