# Grant Resubmission Strategies

## Purpose

Resubmission is a normal and expected part of the grant funding process. Most funded grants (across agencies) were not funded on first submission. This guide provides practical strategies for resubmission across US and CN funding agencies.

## NIH A1 Resubmission

### Overview

NIH allows one resubmission (A1) of an unfunded application. The A1 must be submitted within 37 months of the original (A0) submission date. If the A1 is not funded, you must submit a substantially different "new" (A0) application.

### The 1-Page Introduction

The A1 resubmission includes a 1-page "Introduction to Revised Application" that appears before the Research Strategy. This is arguably the most important page in the resubmission.

**Format requirements:**
- Maximum 1 page
- Must summarize substantial additions, deletions, and changes
- Must respond to the critiques from the Summary Statement
- Changes in the Research Strategy should be marked (bold, italics, or underline - pick one and be consistent)

**Writing the introduction effectively:**

1. **Open with a brief framing statement** (1-2 sentences):
   "We thank the reviewers for their thoughtful evaluation. We have substantially revised the application to address all major concerns, as summarized below."

2. **Organize by reviewer concern, not by reviewer:**
   Instead of "Reviewer 1 said X, Reviewer 2 said Y," group by topic: "Regarding concerns about sample size (Reviewers 1 and 3)..."

3. **For each concern, use this structure:**
   - **Concern:** [Brief paraphrase - 1 sentence]
   - **Response:** [What you did about it - 2-3 sentences]
   - **Location:** [Where to find the change - "See page X, paragraph Y"]

4. **Show, don't just tell:**
   - Wrong: "We have addressed the sample size concern."
   - Right: "We have increased the proposed sample size from N=50 to N=120, based on a revised power analysis (alpha=0.05, power=0.80, effect size d=0.4) now detailed on page 7."

5. **When you disagree with a reviewer:**
   - Never dismiss the criticism
   - Provide new data or a strong published reference
   - Acknowledge the reviewer's perspective before presenting your rebuttal
   - Frame as: "We appreciate this concern. New data from our lab (Figure 2, now included) demonstrate that [evidence addressing the concern]."

**What NOT to include in the introduction:**
- Defensive or argumentative language
- Line-by-line responses to minor comments (focus on major concerns)
- New aims or major conceptual changes without explanation
- Excuses for why the original proposal was weak

### The 37-Month Window

- The A1 must be submitted within 37 months of the A0's receipt date
- If you miss this window, you must submit as a new A0
- Track your deadline carefully - it is not flexible

### When NOT to Resubmit as an A1

Consider submitting as a new A0 instead if:
- The study section was clearly a poor fit (request reassignment instead)
- The fundamental premise was questioned by multiple reviewers
- Your approach has changed so substantially that it is essentially a different project
- You received a score >45 (percentile) with major concerns about significance or innovation
- The scientific landscape has changed significantly since the A0

### Tips for Strengthening the A1

1. **Add new preliminary data.** This is the single most impactful change. If reviewers questioned feasibility, show data that prove it.

2. **Narrow the scope.** A common A0 problem is overambition. Cutting one aim and deepening the remaining aims is often the right move.

3. **Strengthen the approach.** Add power analyses, alternative approaches, more controls, better statistical plans.

4. **Upgrade the team.** If expertise gaps were noted, add a co-investigator or consultant.

5. **Update the significance.** New publications supporting your rationale should be incorporated.

6. **Request a different study section** if the review was clearly driven by misalignment with reviewer expertise (discuss with your PO first).

## NSF Resubmission

### Overview

NSF does not have a formal resubmission mechanism like NIH's A0/A1 system. Declined proposals can be revised and resubmitted as "new" proposals at any time. However, NSF reviewers and program officers may be aware of previous submissions.

### Key Differences from NIH

- **No formal resubmission introduction.** There is no designated page for responding to prior reviews.
- **No resubmission limit.** You can resubmit as many times as you want.
- **Reviews may not carry forward.** Your resubmission may go to entirely different reviewers.
- **The program officer's perspective matters more.** NSF POs have significant discretion.

### Address Concerns in the Revised Narrative

Since there is no introduction page, you must address prior weaknesses within the proposal itself:

1. **Strengthen the weak sections directly.** If reviewers said the Broader Impacts were weak, make them genuinely stronger - don't just add a paragraph.

2. **You may reference the previous review in a cover letter** (optional). A brief note like "This is a revised version of proposal #XXXXXXX, substantially revised to address reviewer feedback" is appropriate.

3. **Contact the program officer** after receiving the decline. POs will often discuss:
   - Which reviewer concerns were most important
   - Whether the project fits the program
   - Suggestions for strengthening the resubmission
   - Whether a different program might be more appropriate

### Program Officer Consultation

NSF program officers are typically more accessible and directive than NIH POs. After a decline:

1. **Wait 1-2 weeks** after receiving reviews to process the feedback.
2. **Email the PO** with a brief summary of your planned revisions and ask for a phone call.
3. **During the call, ask:**
   - "Which of the reviewer concerns do you consider most critical?"
   - "Is this project appropriate for this program, or would you recommend a different one?"
   - "Do you have suggestions for strengthening the proposal?"
   - "Are there upcoming solicitations that might be a better fit?"
4. **Follow up** with a brief email summarizing the conversation and your planned changes.

### NSF-Specific Strengthening Strategies

- **Broader Impacts:** This is the most common weak point. Go beyond "training students" to include concrete plans for public engagement, broadening participation, and societal benefit.
- **Intellectual Merit:** Ensure the intellectual contributions are clearly stated, not buried in methodology.
- **Budget justification:** NSF reviewers scrutinize budgets more than NIH reviewers. Ensure every line item is clearly tied to the project.
- **Data Management Plan:** Increasingly scrutinized. Make it specific to your project, not generic.

## NSFC 重新申请 (Resubmission)

### 概述

NSFC (国家自然科学基金委员会) 没有像NIH那样的正式"修改后重新提交"机制。未获资助的申请可以在下一个申请周期重新提交。每年3月是主要申请窗口。

### 修改策略

#### 1. 认真研读评审意见

NSFC会提供3-5位函评专家的评审意见。这些意见通常包括：
- 对各评审维度的评分
- 具体文字评述
- 资助建议（优先资助/建议资助/不建议资助）

**分析步骤：**
- 逐条列出每位专家的意见
- 区分"共性问题"（多位专家都提到的）和"个性意见"（仅一位专家提出的）
- 共性问题必须重点解决
- 个性意见也需要认真对待，但优先级可以适当调整

#### 2. 常见退回理由与对策

| 退回理由 | 频率 | 对策 |
|---------|------|------|
| 创新性不足 | ~35% | 重新凝练科学问题，突出原创贡献，明确与已有研究的区别 |
| 研究方案不够详细 | ~25% | 补充详细的技术路线图，增加关键实验的具体方案 |
| 前期基础薄弱 | ~20% | 补充预实验数据，发表相关论文，增加研究基础的积累 |
| 研究内容过多/过于庞大 | ~15% | 精简研究内容，聚焦核心科学问题 |
| 立项依据不充分 | ~15% | 加强文献综述的针对性，明确科学问题的来源和重要性 |
| 申请代码不匹配 | ~5% | 仔细核对学科代码，必要时咨询学科主任 |
| 团队结构不合理 | ~10% | 调整团队成员，补充关键技术方向的合作者 |

#### 3. 修改的核心原则

**必须做到的：**
- 针对每条评审意见都有明确的回应和改进
- 补充新的前期研究数据（这是最有说服力的改进）
- 精炼科学问题的表述，使创新点更加突出
- 在摘要和立项依据中就体现出改进

**需要避免的：**
- 仅做文字层面的修改而不改进实质内容
- 忽视评审意见中提到的关键问题
- 大幅改变研究方向（这样不如作为新申请重新构思）
- 在申请书中直接回应评审意见（NSFC申请书中没有回应评审意见的专门页面）

### 评审意见回应方式

与NIH不同，NSFC申请书中没有专门回应评审意见的页面。修改需要体现在申请书的各个部分中：

1. **立项依据部分：** 如果评审专家质疑了科学问题的重要性，需要加强论证
2. **研究内容与方案：** 如果评审专家指出方案不够详细，需要细化技术路线
3. **创新点：** 如果评审专家认为创新性不足，需要重新凝练创新点的表述
4. **研究基础：** 如果评审专家质疑研究能力，需要补充新的前期成果
5. **可行性分析：** 如果评审专家担心可行性，需要加强论证

**关键提示：** 虽然不能直接在申请书中回应评审意见，但可以在重新提交时通过"项目申请补充说明"功能简要说明修改内容（部分年度和项目类型适用，需查看当年申请要求）。

### 青年基金 (Youth Fund) 特殊注意事项

- 年龄限制：男性未满35周岁，女性未满40周岁（以申请当年1月1日为准）
- 如果接近年龄上限，需要特别重视每次申请机会
- 建议在博士毕业后尽早开始申请
- 青年基金更看重申请人的发展潜力和独立科研能力

### 面上项目 (General Program) 特殊注意事项

- 无年龄限制，但需要更强的前期积累
- 每次申请需要明显的进步，不能"换汤不换药"
- 连续多年未获资助时，考虑以下策略：
  - 更换研究方向或角度
  - 先申请难度较低的项目（如地区基金、联合基金等）积累经验
  - 请已获资助的同行帮助审阅申请书
  - 参加NSFC组织的申请培训

## General Resubmission Principles

These principles apply across all agencies and mechanisms.

### 1. Read Reviews Objectively (The 48-Hour Rule)

**Wait at least 48 hours before reading reviews.** Initial reactions to criticism are emotional and unproductive. After 48 hours:

- Read reviews once without taking notes - just absorb.
- Read a second time and highlight every specific criticism.
- Read a third time and categorize each criticism (see below).
- Discuss with a trusted colleague before deciding how to respond.

**Emotional management:**
- Harsh reviews are normal. Even top scientists receive them.
- The reviewer is evaluating the proposal, not you as a person.
- A critical review that provides actionable feedback is more useful than a vague positive one.
- Channel frustration into motivation for improvement.

### 2. Categorize Feedback

Sort every piece of reviewer feedback into these categories:

**Category A - Must Address (Critical):**
- Concerns raised by 2+ reviewers
- Concerns about the fundamental premise or significance
- Methodological flaws that undermine the approach
- Missing elements that are required by the agency

**Category B - Should Address (Important):**
- Concerns raised by 1 reviewer that have merit
- Suggestions for additional experiments or analyses
- Requests for more detail in specific sections
- Concerns about scope or timeline

**Category C - Consider (Optional):**
- Minor wording suggestions
- Stylistic preferences
- "Nice to have" additions
- Reviewer misunderstandings you can clarify with a sentence

**Category D - Respectfully Rebut:**
- Factual errors by the reviewer
- Criticisms based on outdated information
- Concerns already addressed in the proposal (reviewer missed it)
- Opinions that contradict established consensus

**Important:** Category D should be small. If you find yourself putting most criticisms in Category D, you are not being objective.

### 3. Get External Feedback Before Resubmitting

Before revising, get fresh perspectives:

- **Mock review panel:** Assemble 2-3 colleagues who did NOT read the original. Have them review independently and then discuss.
- **Mentor review:** If you have a senior mentor, ask them to read both the reviews and your planned response.
- **Grants office consultation:** Many institutions have grant-writing consultants. Use them.
- **Study section member:** If you know someone who serves on the relevant study section (and has no conflict of interest), their insight is invaluable.

### 4. Strengthen with New Preliminary Data

New preliminary data is the single most effective improvement in a resubmission.

**Strategy:**
- Identify the 1-2 most critical feasibility concerns from the reviews.
- Design targeted experiments to address those specific concerns.
- You do not need to complete the proposed aims - you need to show feasibility.
- Even small datasets are powerful if they directly address a reviewer's concern.

**Examples of effective new preliminary data:**
- Reviewer said "unclear if this method works in your system" -> Show it works (even N=3)
- Reviewer said "sample size seems insufficient" -> Do a real power analysis with pilot data
- Reviewer said "no evidence the team can do this" -> Publish a methods paper in the interim
- Reviewer said "the model may not be appropriate" -> Show validation data for the model

**Timeline for data generation:**
- NIH A1: You have up to 37 months - use at least 6-12 months generating new data
- NSF: No window limit, but 1-2 cycles of additional data strengthens significantly
- NSFC: One-year cycle between applications - use the time productively

### 5. Show Growth, Not Just Compliance

The best resubmissions do not merely "fix problems" - they demonstrate that the science has progressed.

**Compliance-only approach (weaker):**
- "Reviewer asked for more controls. We added controls."
- "Reviewer asked for a power analysis. Here it is."
- The proposal reads like a patched version of the original.

**Growth approach (stronger):**
- "Reviewer's concern about controls prompted us to rethink our validation strategy. We now propose a comprehensive validation framework that includes [controls] plus [new elements] supported by [new preliminary data]."
- The proposal reads like a more mature, better-developed project.

**How to demonstrate growth:**
- New publications by the PI that advance the project
- New preliminary data that goes beyond what reviewers asked for
- New collaborators who strengthen the team
- A clearer, more focused research plan (scope often decreases in resubmission)
- Updated significance based on new literature
- Evidence of community engagement (conference presentations, invited talks on the topic)

## Resubmission Decision Framework

Use this decision tree when deciding how to proceed after an unfunded application:

```
Received reviews
    |
    v
Wait 48 hours
    |
    v
Read and categorize feedback
    |
    v
Was the fundamental premise/significance questioned by 2+ reviewers?
    |
    +-- YES --> Consider major reconceptualization or new direction
    |           - Is the science still sound? -> Reframe and resubmit
    |           - Has the field moved on? -> Start fresh with new A0
    |
    +-- NO --> Were the primary concerns about Approach/Methods?
                |
                +-- YES --> Standard resubmission
                |           - Add preliminary data
                |           - Strengthen methodology
                |           - Add contingency plans
                |
                +-- NO --> Were the concerns about Investigators/Environment?
                            |
                            +-- YES --> Strengthen team and resubmit
                            |           - Add co-investigators
                            |           - Get letters of support
                            |           - Document institutional resources
                            |
                            +-- NO --> Was the score close to payline?
                                        |
                                        +-- YES --> Minor revisions, strengthen weak points
                                        |
                                        +-- NO --> Discuss with PO before deciding
```

## Agency-Specific Resubmission Checklists

### NIH A1 Checklist
- [ ] Within 37-month window
- [ ] 1-page introduction drafted addressing all major concerns
- [ ] Changes marked in Research Strategy (bold/italics/underline - choose one)
- [ ] New preliminary data added where feasibility was questioned
- [ ] Scope adjusted if overambition was a concern
- [ ] Power analyses added/revised
- [ ] Alternative approaches strengthened
- [ ] Study section assignment discussed with PO
- [ ] Biosketch updated with new publications
- [ ] Budget revised to match any scope changes
- [ ] All formatting requirements still met

### NSF Resubmission Checklist
- [ ] Program officer consulted after decline
- [ ] Broader Impacts substantially strengthened (most common weakness)
- [ ] Intellectual Merit more clearly articulated
- [ ] Cover letter mentions this is a revision of proposal #XXXXXXX
- [ ] Data Management Plan updated
- [ ] Budget justification strengthened
- [ ] New preliminary data or publications incorporated
- [ ] Addressed weak points directly in the narrative

### NSFC 重新申请清单
- [ ] 逐条分析了所有评审意见
- [ ] 共性问题（多位专家提到的）已重点解决
- [ ] 补充了新的前期研究数据
- [ ] 创新点的表述更加清晰和准确
- [ ] 技术路线更加详细和可行
- [ ] 研究内容的范围适当调整
- [ ] 团队结构优化（如需要）
- [ ] 摘要已更新以反映改进
- [ ] 申请代码确认正确
- [ ] 经费预算与修改后的研究内容匹配
- [ ] 请有经验的同行审阅了修改稿

## Common Resubmission Mistakes

Avoid these pitfalls when resubmitting:

1. **Rushing the resubmission.** Take time to generate new data and genuinely improve the proposal. A hasty A1 is worse than waiting and submitting a strong new A0.

2. **Being defensive in the introduction.** Reviewers can detect defensiveness. Approach revisions with gratitude and professionalism.

3. **Making only cosmetic changes.** If the reviews identified substantive problems, substantive changes are needed. Reviewers (especially at NIH where the same study section may review the A1) will notice if changes are superficial.

4. **Changing too much.** A complete overhaul signals that the original idea was fundamentally flawed. Make targeted, substantial improvements while maintaining the core strengths.

5. **Ignoring the "nice" reviewer.** Even positive reviews contain suggestions. Incorporating these shows responsiveness and thoroughness.

6. **Not updating the literature.** Months or years may pass between A0 and A1. Incorporate new relevant publications, especially your own.

7. **Assuming the same reviewers.** At NIH, you may get the same study section but different reviewers. At NSF, it is almost certainly different reviewers. Write for a fresh audience while still addressing prior concerns.

8. **Not consulting the program officer.** This is free expert advice. Use it.
