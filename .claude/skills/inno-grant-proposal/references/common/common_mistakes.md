# Common Grant Proposal Mistakes

## Purpose

This reference catalogs the most frequent mistakes in grant proposals, organized by category. Each entry includes frequency/severity estimates, detection methods, and fixes. Use this as a checklist during proposal review and as a teaching resource.

**Related resources:**
- Scoring rubrics: `../rubrics/nsf_rubric.json`, `../rubrics/nih_rubric.json`, `../rubrics/nsfc_rubric.json`
- Reviewer perspectives: `reviewer_personas.md`
- Resubmission strategies: `resubmission.md`
- NSFC-specific: `../cn/nsfc_guide.md` (Section 13: 形式审查)

## 1. Conceptual Mistakes

These are the most damaging mistakes because they undermine the scientific foundation of the proposal.

### 1.1 Vague Hypothesis

**Frequency:** ~40% of proposals
**Severity:** High - often fatal to funding
**Auto-reject risk:** No, but consistently scores poorly

**What it looks like:**
- "We hypothesize that X plays a role in Y."
- "We will investigate the relationship between A and B."
- "Our preliminary data suggest that X may be involved in Y."

**How to detect:**
- Can you identify independent and dependent variables?
- Is the hypothesis falsifiable with a single experiment?
- Does the hypothesis predict a specific direction of effect?
- Could you design a study to prove the hypothesis wrong?

**How to fix:**
- Rewrite as: "We hypothesize that [specific mechanism] causes [specific outcome] through [specific pathway], as evidenced by [preliminary data]."
- Ensure the hypothesis is testable, falsifiable, and directly tied to the proposed aims.
- Use the "If...then...because..." framework: "If [hypothesis is true], then [predicted observation], because [mechanism]."

---

### 1.2 Generic Innovation Claims

**Frequency:** ~50% of proposals
**Severity:** High - reviewers are particularly sensitive to hollow innovation claims

**What it looks like:**
- "This is the first study to..."
- "Our novel approach will..."
- "This innovative project..."
- Using "novel" or "innovative" as adjectives without substantiation

**How to detect:**
- Remove all instances of "novel," "innovative," "first," "unique." Does the text still convey what is new?
- Is there a comparison to existing approaches with specific advantages listed?
- Can the reviewer identify the innovation without being told it exists?

**How to fix:**
- Replace adjectives with evidence: Instead of "our novel method," write "our method achieves X sensitivity compared to Y sensitivity of current approaches, because of [technical reason]."
- Include a comparison table: Current Approach vs. Proposed Approach, with specific metrics.
- Let the innovation speak for itself through concrete technical details.

---

### 1.3 Misalignment with Agency Mission

**Frequency:** ~15% of proposals
**Severity:** Very High - often triaged immediately

**What it looks like:**
- Submitting basic science to a translational mechanism
- Sending a clinical proposal to a basic science program
- Proposing work outside the funding agency's scope
- Ignoring specific FOA (Funding Opportunity Announcement) requirements

**How to detect:**
- Compare the proposal's specific aims against the FOA or program description line by line.
- Ask: "Would this proposal fit equally well at a different agency?" If yes, it may not be targeted enough.
- Check if the proposal addresses every required review criterion.

**How to fix:**
- Read the FOA three times before writing. Highlight key phrases and requirements.
- Frame the proposal to explicitly address the agency's stated priorities.
- Contact the program officer to confirm fit before submitting.
- For NSFC: verify alignment with the specific 申请代码 (application code) and 资助领域 (funding area).

---

### 1.4 Unclear Significance

**Frequency:** ~30% of proposals
**Severity:** High

**What it looks like:**
- Significance section reads like a literature review
- "This is important because it has not been studied" (novelty is not significance)
- No clear statement of what changes if the project succeeds
- Failure to connect to broader health/societal impact

**How to detect:**
- Can you complete this sentence: "If this project succeeds, [specific field/patient population/technology] will benefit because ___"?
- Is the "so what?" question answered explicitly?
- Does the significance section end with a forward-looking impact statement?

**How to fix:**
- Start the significance section with the problem, not the literature.
- Distinguish between the knowledge gap and why filling it matters.
- Include a concrete "impact statement" at the end of the section.
- Quantify the impact where possible (patients affected, economic cost of the problem, etc.).

---

### 1.5 Non-Measurable Objectives

**Frequency:** ~25% of proposals
**Severity:** Medium-High

**What it looks like:**
- "Aim 1: Understand the role of X in Y"
- "Aim 2: Explore the mechanism of Z"
- "Aim 3: Develop new insights into..."
- Objectives that cannot be evaluated as achieved or not achieved

**How to detect:**
- Apply the "deliverable test": What specific result demonstrates that this aim is complete?
- Can you write a milestone table with binary yes/no completion criteria?
- Would two independent reviewers agree on whether the aim was accomplished?

**How to fix:**
- Rewrite aims with action verbs tied to measurable outcomes: "Determine," "Quantify," "Establish," "Validate," "Demonstrate."
- Add explicit success criteria: "We will consider this aim achieved when [specific measurable outcome]."
- Include milestones with defined endpoints for each aim.

## 2. Writing Mistakes

These mistakes reduce clarity and professionalism, making the reviewer's job harder.

### 2.1 Passive Voice Overuse

**Frequency:** ~60% of proposals (to varying degrees)
**Severity:** Low-Medium (cumulative effect on readability)

**What it looks like:**
- "Experiments will be performed to determine..."
- "It is hypothesized that..."
- "Samples were collected and analyzed by..."
- Entire paragraphs without a clear agent performing actions

**How to detect:**
- Search for "will be," "was/were [verb]ed," "it is," "it was."
- Count passive constructions per paragraph. More than 2 per paragraph is excessive.
- Read aloud - passive-heavy text sounds bureaucratic and disengaged.

**How to fix:**
- "We will perform experiments to determine..." or better: "We will determine [outcome] by [method]."
- "We hypothesize that..."
- Use active voice for at least 70% of sentences.
- Reserve passive voice for cases where the agent is truly unimportant or unknown.

---

### 2.2 Jargon Without Definition

**Frequency:** ~35% of proposals
**Severity:** Medium - can alienate non-specialist reviewers on the panel

**What it looks like:**
- Using field-specific acronyms without first defining them
- Assuming all reviewers share the applicant's sub-specialty knowledge
- Technical terms used without context

**How to detect:**
- Have someone outside your immediate sub-field read the first two pages. Can they follow?
- List all acronyms and technical terms. Is each defined at first use?
- Check if the abstract is accessible to any scientist in the broad field.

**How to fix:**
- Define every acronym at first use. Re-define at the start of each major section in long proposals.
- For NIH: assume reviewers are biomedical scientists but not necessarily in your sub-field.
- For NSFC: remember that 函评专家 (mail reviewers) may be in adjacent fields.
- Include a brief explanatory phrase after technical terms: "chromatin immunoprecipitation (ChIP), a method for identifying protein-DNA interactions."

---

### 2.3 Missing Topic Sentences

**Frequency:** ~45% of proposals
**Severity:** Medium - significantly impacts scanability

**What it looks like:**
- Paragraphs that start with background details instead of the main point
- The key message is buried in the middle or end of the paragraph
- Reviewers have to read the entire paragraph to understand its purpose

**How to detect:**
- Read only the first sentence of each paragraph in the Research Strategy. Can you reconstruct the argument?
- If not, the topic sentences are missing or weak.

**How to fix:**
- Write the first sentence of each paragraph as a mini-abstract of that paragraph.
- Use the pattern: [Claim/Point]. [Evidence]. [Implication for the proposal].
- Bold key topic sentences in the Research Strategy (this is acceptable and recommended for NIH/NSF).

---

### 2.4 Inconsistent Terminology

**Frequency:** ~25% of proposals
**Severity:** Medium - creates confusion and suggests carelessness

**What it looks like:**
- Switching between "participants," "subjects," and "patients" for the same population
- Using different names for the same method or construct
- Aim titles in the Specific Aims page differ from aim titles in the Research Strategy

**How to detect:**
- Create a terminology glossary before writing. Stick to it.
- Use search-and-replace to check consistency of key terms.
- Cross-reference aim titles, figure references, and section headers.

**How to fix:**
- Choose one term per concept and use it throughout.
- If a concept has a common abbreviation, define it once and use the abbreviation consistently.
- Proofread specifically for terminology consistency as a separate editing pass.

---

### 2.5 AI-Flavored Language Patterns

**Frequency:** ~15% and rising rapidly
**Severity:** Medium-High - increasingly noticed by reviewers and may trigger skepticism

**What it looks like:**
- Overuse of "delve into," "leverage," "utilize," "cutting-edge," "groundbreaking"
- Formulaic paragraph structures with identical transition patterns
- Unnaturally smooth prose that lacks the PI's authentic voice
- Lists of three adjectives before every noun ("comprehensive, innovative, and transformative")
- "In the rapidly evolving landscape of..."
- "This paradigm-shifting approach..."

**How to detect:**
- Read the text aloud. Does it sound like the PI talking to a colleague, or like a press release?
- Check for the known AI-marker words: "delve," "cutting-edge," "landscape," "realm," "multifaceted," "holistic," "synergy," "paradigm"
- Look for suspiciously uniform paragraph lengths and transition patterns.

**How to fix:**
- Write the first draft yourself, then use AI for editing only.
- If using AI for drafting, heavily revise to inject your voice and specific technical details.
- Replace generic superlatives with specific, quantified claims.
- Have a colleague read it and flag anything that "doesn't sound like you."
- Use AI as a thinking partner, not a ghostwriter.

## 3. Technical Mistakes

These mistakes undermine confidence in the proposed research plan.

### 3.1 Insufficient Methodological Detail

**Frequency:** ~35% of proposals
**Severity:** High

**What it looks like:**
- "Standard techniques will be used for..."
- "Data will be analyzed using appropriate statistical methods."
- "We will use established protocols for [complex procedure]."
- Methods sections that read like a table of contents instead of a protocol

**How to detect:**
- Could a competent researcher in your field replicate the proposed experiments from your description?
- Are specific kits, software versions, instrument models, or protocol parameters mentioned?
- Are statistical tests named with justification for their selection?

**How to fix:**
- Name specific methods, kits, software, and parameters.
- Include enough detail that a reviewer can evaluate feasibility.
- For statistical analysis: name the test, state the null hypothesis, specify alpha level, and provide power calculation.
- Balance: don't turn the proposal into a protocol paper, but provide enough detail for evaluation.

---

### 3.2 Overambitious Scope

**Frequency:** ~30% of proposals
**Severity:** High - one of the most common reasons for poor scores

**What it looks like:**
- 4-5 specific aims for a standard grant
- Each aim could be its own funded project
- Timeline shows every aim running simultaneously
- The proposal promises to solve an entire field's problems

**How to detect:**
- Count the number of distinct experimental systems, techniques, and analyses.
- Estimate realistic person-hours for each aim. Does it fit in the budget?
- Ask: "Could a reasonable PI complete 80% of this in the funded period?"

**How to fix:**
- Reduce to 2-3 focused aims that clearly build on each other.
- Move ambitious extensions to "Future Directions."
- Show depth over breadth - a thorough study of one question beats a superficial survey of five.
- For NSFC: 研究内容 should be focused; 3-4 研究内容 items maximum for 面上项目.

---

### 3.3 Missing Preliminary Data

**Frequency:** ~20% of proposals (varies by mechanism)
**Severity:** High for R01/面上; Lower for R21/青年基金

**What it looks like:**
- Claims of feasibility without supporting data
- "Based on the literature, we expect..."
- Preliminary data from a different system/model/population
- Preliminary data that is underpowered or unconvincing

**How to detect:**
- For each key claim or assumption, ask: "What is the evidence?"
- Map each aim to its supporting preliminary data. Are there gaps?
- Is the preliminary data from the PI's own lab, or is it borrowed from the literature?

**How to fix:**
- Generate key preliminary data before submitting (often worth delaying one cycle).
- If using literature-based rationale, acknowledge this explicitly and explain why it supports feasibility.
- For exploratory mechanisms (R21, R03, 青年基金): clearly frame as hypothesis-generating.
- Include pilot data figures even if small - they demonstrate technical competence.

---

### 3.4 No Contingency Plans

**Frequency:** ~40% of proposals
**Severity:** Medium-High

**What it looks like:**
- No mention of what happens if an experiment fails
- No alternative approaches discussed
- "We do not anticipate difficulties because..."
- Risk is acknowledged but no mitigation is proposed

**How to detect:**
- For each aim, ask: "What is the most likely failure mode?"
- Is there a Plan B for each technically challenging experiment?
- Are the contingency plans genuine alternatives, not just weaker versions of Plan A?

**How to fix:**
- Add a "Potential Pitfalls and Alternative Approaches" section (or integrate into each aim).
- For each risk: state the risk, its likelihood, its impact, and the specific alternative.
- Show that you have thought deeply about what could go wrong.
- Make contingency plans concrete: "If approach A fails due to [specific reason], we will use approach B, which we have validated in [preliminary data reference]."

---

### 3.5 Budget-Activity Misalignment

**Frequency:** ~20% of proposals
**Severity:** Medium (but can raise red flags)

**What it looks like:**
- Expensive equipment requested but not described in the methods
- Large travel budget with no justification in the research plan
- Personnel effort doesn't match the workload described
- Key consumables or services not budgeted

**How to detect:**
- Walk through each aim and list every resource needed. Is each budgeted?
- Walk through the budget and check that every item is justified in the research plan.
- Check that personnel effort percentages are realistic for the work described.

**How to fix:**
- Build the budget bottom-up from the research plan, not top-down from a target number.
- Include brief budget justification notes that reference specific aims.
- Ensure personnel effort matches their described contributions.
- For NSFC: follow the 经费预算 categories strictly and ensure 设备费, 材料费, etc., match the proposed work.

## 4. Formatting Mistakes

These are often the easiest to fix but can be the most costly when violated.

### 4.1 Page Limit Violations

**Frequency:** ~5% of submissions
**Severity:** CRITICAL - auto-reject at most agencies

**What it looks like:**
- NIH Research Strategy exceeding 12 pages (R01) or 6 pages (R21)
- NSF Project Description exceeding 15 pages
- NSFC exceeding the designated page/character limits
- "Creative" workarounds like reducing font size or margins

**How to detect:**
- Convert to PDF and count pages using the exact formatting requirements.
- Check with the agency's submission system (eRA Commons, Research.gov, NSFC system) before the deadline.

**How to fix:**
- Know the limits before you start writing. Set up the document template first.
- Build in a "cut budget" of 10% - write to 90% of the limit, use the remaining 10% for figures and emphasis.
- Have a colleague identify the least essential 15% of content. Cut it.
- Never rely on last-minute formatting tricks - they are usually detected.

---

### 4.2 Font/Margin Non-Compliance

**Frequency:** ~8% of submissions
**Severity:** High - can trigger administrative withdrawal

**What it looks like:**
- Using 11pt font when 11pt minimum is required (some use 10.5pt)
- Margins less than 0.5 inches
- Using a non-approved font (NIH accepts Arial, Helvetica, Palatino, Georgia, and others at 11pt)
- Figures with text smaller than the minimum font size

**How to detect:**
- Use the agency's formatting checker tool.
- Print a test page and measure margins physically.
- Check figure text size - it must also comply with font minimums.

**How to fix:**
- Use the agency-provided template if available.
- Set up document styles once and never override them.
- For figures: ensure all text within figures meets the minimum size requirement (commonly 11pt for NIH).
- Check the most current formatting guidelines - they change occasionally.

---

### 4.3 Missing Required Sections

**Frequency:** ~10% of submissions
**Severity:** High - may trigger "incomplete application" status

**What it looks like:**
- NIH: Missing Rigor and Reproducibility, Authentication of Key Biological Resources, or Vertebrate Animals section
- NSF: Missing Data Management Plan, Mentoring Plan (if applicable), or Broader Impacts
- NSFC: Missing required sections like 创新点, 可行性分析, or 研究队伍
- Letters of support not included when collaborators are named

**How to detect:**
- Use the FOA's checklist (every FOA has one).
- Cross-reference with the agency's application guide.
- Have an experienced colleague or grants office review the complete package.

**How to fix:**
- Create a master checklist from the FOA before starting.
- Check off each required element as it is completed.
- Submit a draft to your grants office at least 1 week before the deadline for administrative review.

---

### 4.4 Poor Figure Quality

**Frequency:** ~25% of proposals
**Severity:** Medium - signals lack of professionalism and can obscure data

**What it looks like:**
- Low-resolution images (especially screenshots of graphs)
- Missing axis labels or legends
- Figures that are too small to read
- Color-only figures that are indistinguishable in grayscale (some reviewers print in B&W)
- Overly complex figures that try to convey too much

**How to detect:**
- Print the proposal at 100% and check if every figure element is legible.
- View figures at 150% zoom on screen - any pixelation?
- Show figures to someone unfamiliar with the project. Can they understand the main point in 30 seconds?

**How to fix:**
- Create figures at 300+ DPI for print quality.
- Use vector graphics (PDF, SVG, EPS) when possible.
- Every figure needs: title, axis labels, legend, scale bars (for images), and a descriptive caption.
- Design figures to be self-explanatory - a reviewer should understand them without reading the text.
- Use consistent visual style across all figures.

## 5. Strategic Mistakes

These mistakes reflect poor planning rather than poor science.

### 5.1 Wrong Program Selection

**Frequency:** ~10% of submissions
**Severity:** Very High - wasted effort

**What it looks like:**
- Submitting an R01-scope project to an R21 mechanism
- Applying to a program with a disease focus when you study basic biology
- Choosing the wrong NSFC 申请代码 (application code)
- Applying to a mechanism you are not eligible for (career stage, institution type)

**How to detect:**
- Read the mechanism eligibility requirements carefully.
- Compare your project scope to what the mechanism typically funds (check NIH Reporter, NSF Award Search, or NSFC funded project lists).
- Contact the program officer before submitting.

**How to fix:**
- Research mechanisms thoroughly before committing to one.
- Talk to the program officer - this is expected and welcomed.
- Look at who has been funded by this mechanism in the last 3-5 years.
- For NSFC: carefully review the 学科代码 descriptions and consult with funded colleagues.

---

### 5.2 Weak Team Composition

**Frequency:** ~15% of proposals
**Severity:** Medium-High

**What it looks like:**
- PI lacks expertise in key methods proposed
- No collaborators for interdisciplinary work
- Named consultants with no letters of support
- Team listed but roles not defined
- All team members are from the same lab/department

**How to detect:**
- For each proposed method, can you identify who on the team has published using it?
- Are there gaps in expertise that no team member covers?
- Are collaborator roles and commitments specific and documented?

**How to fix:**
- Recruit collaborators to fill expertise gaps. Genuine collaboration strengthens proposals.
- Include letters of support from all key personnel and collaborators.
- Define specific roles: who does what, when, and how much effort.
- For multi-PI proposals: clearly delineate leadership and decision-making structure.
- For NSFC: ensure 研究队伍 section lists specific contributions for each member.

---

### 5.3 Late Submission

**Frequency:** ~5% (of intended submissions - many never submit at all)
**Severity:** CRITICAL - no proposal, no funding

**What it looks like:**
- Rushing to complete the proposal in the final week
- Skipping internal review processes due to time pressure
- Submission system errors discovered at 4:59 PM on deadline day
- Missing institutional sign-offs

**How to detect:**
- Is there a written timeline working backward from the deadline?
- Is the first complete draft done at least 2 weeks before submission?
- Has the grants office been engaged early?

**How to fix:**
- Work backward from the deadline. Key milestones:
  - 8 weeks before: Aims page complete, shared with mentors/colleagues
  - 6 weeks before: First draft of Research Strategy
  - 4 weeks before: Complete draft for internal review
  - 2 weeks before: Revised draft, budget finalized
  - 1 week before: Final package to grants office for submission
- Build in buffer time. Something always goes wrong.
- Submit at least 24-48 hours early. Systems crash on deadline day.

---

### 5.4 Ignoring Reviewer Feedback in Resubmission

**Frequency:** ~20% of resubmissions
**Severity:** Very High - nearly guarantees another rejection

**What it looks like:**
- Resubmission intro that dismisses reviewer concerns
- "We disagree with the reviewer" without compelling evidence
- Addressing the letter of criticism but not actually changing the proposal
- Making only cosmetic changes and resubmitting essentially the same proposal

**How to detect:**
- Map each reviewer concern to a specific change in the revised proposal.
- Can you point to the exact page and paragraph where each concern is addressed?
- Has someone who read the original reviews also read the revision to verify concerns are addressed?

**How to fix:**
- Address every concern, even ones you disagree with.
- For disagreements: provide new data or a compelling published reference, not just an argument.
- Show changes clearly (bold, italics, or tracked changes in the introduction).
- See the Resubmission guide (resubmission.md) for detailed strategies.

---

### 5.5 Not Consulting the Program Officer

**Frequency:** ~50% of applicants never contact the PO
**Severity:** Medium - missed opportunity

**What it looks like:**
- Submitting without confirming the proposal fits the program
- Not knowing about upcoming changes to review criteria or funding priorities
- Missing information about special funding opportunities or supplements

**How to detect:**
- Have you emailed or called the program officer?
- Do you know the PO's name and current priorities?

**How to fix:**
- Contact the PO with a brief (1-paragraph) summary of your project and ask if it fits.
- POs are generally helpful and want to fund good science in their portfolio.
- For NIH: POs can suggest better study sections or mechanisms.
- For NSF: POs can redirect you to more appropriate programs.
- For NSFC: consult 学科主任 if unsure about the appropriate 申请代码.

## Pre-Submission Checklist

Use this as a final check before submission:

- [ ] Hypothesis is specific, testable, and falsifiable
- [ ] Innovation is demonstrated, not just claimed
- [ ] Proposal aligns with the specific FOA/program requirements
- [ ] Significance answers "so what?" with concrete impact
- [ ] All objectives are measurable with defined success criteria
- [ ] Writing is primarily active voice with defined terms
- [ ] Methods are detailed enough for feasibility evaluation
- [ ] Scope is realistic for the budget and timeline
- [ ] Preliminary data supports each key claim
- [ ] Contingency plans exist for each major risk
- [ ] Budget matches the proposed activities
- [ ] All formatting requirements are met
- [ ] All required sections are included
- [ ] Figures are high-quality and self-explanatory
- [ ] Correct program/mechanism selected
- [ ] Team has all needed expertise documented
- [ ] Program officer has been consulted
- [ ] Submission is planned 48+ hours before deadline
- [ ] Internal review has been completed
